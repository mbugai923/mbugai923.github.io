Healthy Desserts

modified by Maddie Bugai

Purpose

On my journey for a healthier lifestyle, I knew I didn't want to give up the desserts I love. As I got more into gym culture, I felt pressure to follow a strict diet regimen in addition to my physical one. Influencers, body builders, and folks at the gym mention creatine and protein powder and dietary suppliments they swear by. As someone with a rocky relationship with food, I knew calories counting and tracking my carbs, fat, and protein wasn't the way to go. If I did, I would probably fall into unheathy eating habits and restrict myself. However, with my fitness goals, as well as my goal to be healthier in general, I knew that eating sweets whenever I wanted was not the brightest decision. So, I decided to work on modifying some of my favorite recipes to be healthier with the same great taste. I hope that these recipes can be useful for anyone, no matter what their fitness goals are.

Healthy Conversions:

When baking (and cooking), I try to use as many alternatives as I can. However, some of these alternatives are expensive compared to the originals. Whether you choose to use one or all of these alternatives, I hope that this list can help you.

Sugar → Monk Fruit, or another artificial sweetener like Stevia

I prefer monk fruit because it has anti–inflammatory properties, while Stevia can cause digestive problems and/or allergic reactions. Monk fruit is a zero calorie sweetener from the Luo Han Guo plant, which is said to be 250 times sweeter than sucrose.

Brown Sugar → Monk Fruit (or artificial sweetener) + Molasses, Brown Coconut Sugar, or any artificial sweetener labeled as brown sugar, like Truvia

I prefer to make my own brown sugar because it allows me to create the consistency I prefer. In addition, it allows me to make either dark or light brown sugar. I prefer to make it rather than buying an alternative because I believe it is a safer, and healthier option.

Cream Cheese → Cottage Cheese

This may sound weird, but blended cottage cheese produces an almost identical flavor to cream cheese when added to recipes. In addition, it has an insane amount of protein for very few calories. I usually add some fat free cream cheese in my desserts that need more hold to them (as you’ll see in my cheesecake recipe). This ensures that the dessert won’t be too runny.

Sour Cream → Plain Fat–Free Greek Yogurt

You may know that many recipes call for sour cream to add moisture to the dessert. However, sour cream is calorie dense and has high amounts of fat. Plain greek yogurt—fat–free or not—is low in calories, and produces the same effect in cakes that sour cream does without all the calories. Make sure to use plain yogurt instead of vanilla, as this can affect the flavor of your cake.

All Purpose Flour → Almond Flour, Oat Flour, Coconut Flour, etc…

Many people prefer almond flour over other types of flour because it is low calorie. However, almond flour has more moisture than other types of flour; so, when you use it in recipes, make sure to add a little extra. I use oat flour because it is easy to make (just blend some oats in a blender or food processor), and because it is very cheap in comparison to almond or coconut flour. It also has more protein. Unfortunately, it has more calories, and produces a denser dessert. It all depends on what you prefer.

Butter, Oil → Avocado Oil, or Vegan Butter

Avocado oil and vegan butter are rich in monounsaturated fats, which can help improve heart health. Avocado oil also contains vitamins E and K, as well as various beneficial antioxidants.

Desserts

Protein Packed Classic Cheesecake

Crust:

  • 1½ cups graham cracker crumbs
  • 2 tablespoons brown sugar (monk fruit + molasses)
  • 7 tablespoons avocado oil (or melted butter)
  • 1 teaspoon cinnamon (optional)

Middle:

  • 24 ounces nonfat cottage cheese
  • 8 ounces fat free cream cheese
  • 2 tablespoons Jello Cheesecake powder
  • 1 cup monk fruit
  • ½ cup plain nonfat greek yogurt
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 2 teaspoons lemon juice (optional)
  • 3 large eggs

Instructions:

  1. Crust: Preheat oven to 350℉. Crush graham crackers either in a food processor, blender, or by hand. There should be no big chunks of crumbs. Add brown sugar and cinnamon. Mix until combined. Add oil (or melted butter) and mix until mixture is sandy. Spread crust evenly in an ungreased springform pan (either 9in or 10in). Apply medium pressure to make the crust compact. If desired, push the crust up the edges of the pan. Bake for 10 minutes. Take out to cool.
  2. Middle: Blend cottage cheese in a blender or use an immersion blender until curds are smooth. Transfer into a large mixing bowl. Add room temperature cream cheese and monk fruit. Using a mixer on medium-high speed, beat the mixture until creamy (2 minutes). The mixture may be thinner than usual cheesecake batter. Add Cheesecake Jello powder and mix until combined. Add yogurt, vanilla extract, and lemon juice, and beat until combined. On medium speed, add room temperature eggs one at a time and blend until just combined. Avoid over mixing.
  3. Prepare a water bath: Boil enough water to reach an inch up from the bottom of the springform pan. While the water is heating up, wrap the springform pan in aluminum foil to prevent water from leaking into the cake. Place the pan inside a large roasting pan and transfer batter into the springform pan. Even out with a rubber spatula. Carefully pour hot water into the roasting pan. Carefully transfer both pans into the oven.
  4. Bake: Bake for 55-70 minutes. The middle should wobble slightly when fully cooked. Turn the oven off, and place a wooden spoon between the oven door to keep the oven door ajar. Let cheesecake cool in the oven for 1 hour. It will finish cooking during this time. Take cheesecake out and cool until room temperature. Transfer cheese to the fridge for 4 hours or overnight (suggested).
  5. Remove from pan: Use a knife to loosen the sides of the cheesecake from the pan before removing the rim. For clean slices, run the knife under warm water between each slice.
  6. Serve: Serve with desired toppings and store in the fridge for up to 5 days.

Protein Brownies

Dry Ingredients:

  • ½ cup chocolate protein powder
  • ½ cup almond or oat flour
  • ⅓ cup cocoa powder
  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • ½ cup semi sweet chocolate chips

Wet Ingredients:

  • 2 teaspoons vanilla extract
  • ¼ cup plain nonfat greek yogurt
  • ½ cup melted butter or avocado oil
  • 1 cup monk fruit or other artificial sweetener

Instructions:

  1. Preheat oven to 350℉. Spray or line a 9x9 pan.
  2. Mix dry ingredients in a small bowl.
  3. In a larger bowl, mix melted butter with sugar substitute until smooth, 30 seconds.
  4. Add in yogurt and vanilla extract and mix until smooth, 1 minute.
  5. Add dry ingredients ⅓ at a time, making sure to mix all the way through before adding more. Add chocolate chips and stir until just combined. If batter is too thick, add a splash of milk. If too thin, add a few teaspoons of almond flour.
  6. Poor into pan and smooth out. Bake for 30 minutes or until toothpick comes out clean.
  7. Let cool for 30 minutes before slicing and serving.

Protein Classic Chocolate Chip Cookies

Dry Ingredients:

  • 1¾ cups almond flour (or 1½ cups oat flour)
  • ⅓ cup vanilla protein powder
  • ½ teaspoon baking soda
  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • ½ cup semi-sweet chocolate chips (sugar free optional)
  • ½ teaspoon sea salt to top with (optional)

Wet Ingredients:

  • ⅓ cup blended cottage cheese
  • ⅓ cup room temperature butter
  • 1 large egg
  • teaspoon vanilla extract
  • ¼ cup monk fruit
  • ¼ cup brown sugar (monk fruit + molasses)

Instructions:

  1. Preheat oven to 350℉.
  2. Combine dry ingredients in a small bowl, except for the sea salt.
  3. Blend cottage cheese in a blender or use an immersion blender until curds are smooth. In a large mixing bowl, add butter, monk fruit, brown sugar, and vanilla extract. Blend with a hand or stand mixer until creamy. Add blended cottage cheese and mix until smooth. Add egg and mix until just combined.
  4. Add dry ingredients ⅓ at a time, folding the mixture with a rubber spatula until combined.
  5. Add in chocolate chips and fold until mixed.
  6. Using a spoon, grab just over a tablespoon of dough. Roll between greased hands and place on a baking sheet. Cookies should be spaced about an inch apart. Dough yields 14-16 cookies.
  7. Bake for 11-14 minutes or until cookies are golden brown. Sprinkle with sea salt (optional).
  8. Cool for five minutes, then transfer to a wire rack.
  9. Cool an additional 30 minutes before serving.
